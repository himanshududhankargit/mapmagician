What's actually possible

3-point linear vs 5-point TPS

Linear / Affine · few points

One rule for the whole map

With 3 points you can solve a linear (similarity) fit — shift, rotate and one uniform scale. The map stays perfectly rigid; angles and shape are untouched.

Affine (also 3 points) adds shear and separate X/Y scaling: straight lines stay straight, parallels stay parallel, but squares can lean into parallelograms.

Crucially, the same equation applies everywhere. It can't fix local distortion — if the old map shrank more in one corner, linear/affine simply can't reach it. The error gets spread across all points and RMSE rarely hits zero.

TPS · 5+ points

A rubber sheet pinned at each point

Thin Plate Spline treats the map as a flexible sheet. It bends locally to pass through every control point exactly — so with 5 well-placed points the map matches all five with essentially zero residual.

This is why TPS is better for old, warped, hand-drawn or unevenly shrunk maps: real historic distortion isn't uniform, and only a locally-bending model can follow it.

It minimises "bending energy" — it's the smoothest possible surface that still hits all your points, so it doesn't wobble more than it has to.

The drawback of TPS

Flexibility cuts both ways

Because TPS bends to hit every point exactly, it trusts your points completely — including their mistakes:

• A bad control point poisons the area around it. One mis-clicked point and the sheet warps violently nearby to obey it.

• Wild extrapolation outside the points. Beyond the spread of your control points there's nothing pinning the sheet, so it can fold or balloon unpredictably.

• Zero residual is seductive. RMSE of 0 doesn't mean the map is correct — it means it perfectly hit the points you gave it, errors included. Affine's honest leftover error is sometimes more trustworthy. (Switch to TPS above and drag one point far away to see the surrounding grid tear.)

"Linear asks the whole map to compromise. TPS obeys every point — and inherits every mistake you make."

Pick the transformation that matches your source: rigid modern data loves affine; a cracked 1890s estate plan needs TPS — handled with care, and with control points you trust.
